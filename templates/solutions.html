Core Logic

A comprehensive guide to Disk Scheduling Algorithms

01. FCFS (First-Come, First-Served)

FCFS is the simplest disk scheduling algorithm. It services requests strictly in the order they arrive in the I/O queue.

Seek Distance = |Initial - R1| + |R1 - R2| + ... + |Rn-1 - Rn|
  • Pros: Zero overhead; every request is handled fairly without priority logic.
  • Cons: Suffers from the "Convoy Effect" where large seek movements occur between distant tracks.

02. SSTF (Shortest Seek Time First)

SSTF selects the request that is physically closest to the current position of the disk head, regardless of the order of arrival.

  • Pros: Significantly reduces total seek time compared to FCFS.
  • Cons: Leads to Starvation. If new requests keep arriving near the head, distant requests may never be serviced.

03. SCAN & LOOK (Elevator)

These algorithms mimic the behavior of a building elevator. The head moves in one direction, servicing all requests until it hits a limit, then reverses.

SCAN

The head travels to the very edge of the disk (Track 0 or 199) before reversing, even if no request exists there.

LOOK

An optimized version of SCAN. The head "looks" ahead and reverses immediately after servicing the last request in that direction.

04. C-SCAN & C-LOOK (Circular)

To provide a more uniform waiting time, circular versions do not service requests in the reverse direction. Instead, they "jump" back to the start.

Jump Distance (C-SCAN) = |199 - 0| (Max Seek)
  • C-SCAN: Moves to end, jumps to 0, and starts again. Provides extremely uniform wait times.
  • C-LOOK: Moves to the highest request, jumps to the lowest request, and continues. Most efficient for high-density requests.
